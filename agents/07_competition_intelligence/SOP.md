# SOP — Competition Intelligence

## 1. Confirm the decision and scope

1. Read accepted Market Opportunity, Product Intelligence, Buyer Intelligence and Pricing Intelligence reports.
2. Read relevant Manufacturer Hunter and Due Diligence evidence.
3. Define the exact Turkish market-entry decision.
4. Define product variants, applications, buyer types, price tier and geographic scope.
5. Define direct, indirect, substitute, in-house and do-nothing alternatives.
6. If the arena is ambiguous, stop and request clarification.

## 2. Build the evidence register

For every market claim record:

- VERIFIED / ESTIMATED / UNKNOWN
- Claim
- Source
- Date
- Product/HS scope
- Geography
- Method
- Range
- Confidence
- Decision impact

Do not calculate market structure before scope and evidence states are clear.

## 3. Size the relevant opportunity

1. Collect import value and volume for defensible product/HS scope.
2. Identify local production and exports.
3. Separate import dependency from market size.
4. Define addressable, serviceable and achievable initial opportunity.
5. Use ranges when product scope is broader than the selected opportunity.
6. Explain every estimate.
7. Record exact unknowns and how they affect entry.

## 4. Map local manufacturing

1. Identify Turkish manufacturers with relevant own production.
2. Verify factory and product evidence.
3. Map technical range, certification, service and positioning.
4. Distinguish local production from assembly, private label and distribution.
5. Assess where local producers are strong or vulnerable.
6. Do not infer market share from visibility.

## 5. Map international brands and channels

1. Identify major international brands relevant to the exact application.
2. Verify Turkey subsidiaries, offices and employees.
3. Verify authorized distributors and representatives from first-party sources.
4. Separate authorized channels from independent resellers and listings.
5. Map exclusivity, geography and product scope when public.
6. Identify system-integrator and EPC influence.
7. Record UNKNOWN where authorization cannot be confirmed.

## 6. Map substitutes and buyer alternatives

1. Identify direct technical substitutes.
2. Identify lower-cost and premium alternatives.
3. Identify repair, refurbishment and in-house alternatives.
4. Identify “do nothing” or defer-purchase behavior.
5. Compare lifecycle cost, performance, lead time and switching.
6. Map which alternative each buyer segment actually considers.

## 7. Analyze pricing pressure and profit pool

1. Use accepted Pricing Intelligence evidence.
2. Compare market, distributor and competitor prices only on equivalent bases.
3. Assess discounting, tender pressure, service bundling and credit.
4. Identify where profit sits: product, service, consumables, project engineering or availability.
5. Distinguish asking from achieved prices.
6. Identify price-war and margin-compression risk.

## 8. Analyze concentration, loyalty and buying behavior

1. Estimate concentration only from sourced data or label UNKNOWN.
2. Identify approved-vendor, framework, OEM and installed-base effects.
3. Map who specifies and approves brands.
4. Assess customer loyalty and reasons for switching.
5. Assess sales cycle by buyer type.
6. Identify service, stock, reference and credit expectations.
7. Record evidence and confidence.

## 9. Analyze regional demand

1. Map demand by industrial region, city, cluster and buyer type.
2. Link regional demand to verified facilities and applications.
3. Identify logistics, service and travel implications.
4. Avoid equating company count with demand.
5. Identify a practical first-entry geography.

## 10. Identify market and entry barriers

Assess:

- Regulation and certification
- Vendor registration
- Local references
- Incumbent relationships
- Channel exclusivity
- Stock and lead time
- Service and technical support
- Language
- Credit and payment
- Tender access
- Switching cost
- Brand recognition

Classify each barrier as low, medium, high or UNKNOWN and identify a mitigation.

## 11. Evaluate six entry models

For Greenfield, Representation, Commission, Hybrid, Back-to-back and Stock:

1. Define entry thesis.
2. Define buyer value.
3. Define manufacturer value.
4. Define operational requirements.
5. Assess capital and speed.
6. Assess channel and service fit.
7. Assess risks and evidence gaps.
8. Assign viability.
9. Identify the winning conditions.

## 12. Build evidence-linked SWOT

1. Strengths: Atlas/internal advantages for the selected model.
2. Weaknesses: Atlas/internal limitations.
3. Opportunities: external market gaps.
4. Threats: external competitive risks.
5. Link each item to evidence or label ESTIMATED/UNKNOWN.
6. Avoid generic statements.

## 13. Score market entry

1. Score raw Competition Intensity.
2. Score raw Entry Difficulty.
3. Score Market Attractiveness.
4. Score Growth Potential.
5. Score Differentiation Opportunity.
6. Score Profitability.
7. Calculate inverse burden scores.
8. Calculate Atlas Score.
9. Score Evidence Quality separately.
10. Explain evidence, confidence and sensitivity for every score.

## 14. Challenge the preferred strategy

1. State the strongest incumbent response.
2. Test price, lead-time, service and reference disadvantages.
3. Test whether the proposed differentiation matters to buyers.
4. Test whether channel partners already cover the gap.
5. Test whether quick wins create durable business.
6. Rescore when the challenge changes evidence.

## 15. Apply decision framework

- GO only when every gate passes and a viable entry strategy exists.
- WATCH when bounded evidence could change the decision.
- NO-GO when no credible win condition exists.

Do not average away a fatal channel, service, stock or profitability barrier.

## 16. Write and synchronize outputs

1. Write the next `intelligence/competition/RXXX.md`.
2. End with the six mandatory sections in order.
3. Update competition fields in `database/products.csv`.
4. Update Turkey competitive-presence fields in `database/manufacturers.csv`.
5. Update `workspace/active_project.md` with strategy, status and next gate.
6. Update only Competition Intelligence-owned dashboard values.
7. Preserve stable IDs, prior evidence and unrelated content.
8. Cite report ID and verification date.
9. Route GO to Atlas CEO for controlled entry planning.

## Escalation

Escalate sanctions, competition-law concerns, deceptive-research requests, sensitive infrastructure, private data, legal interpretations or any proposed external contact not authorized.

## Completion definition

Work is complete when Atlas can explain not only who competes, but why the market is or is not enterable, where Atlas can win, which model should be used and what evidence could still reverse the decision.
